Visual Toolbox

A significant amount of social infrastructure has been organized under the assumption that all members of society have full vision. Small visual displays; menus, buttons, and icons that require sight; as well as colour-coded user interfaces, present daily challenges to individuals with visual impairments. Visual toolbox was developed at Queen's University by a team of four as a design project for the second-year computer engineering course, APSC-200. The application interface was designed with the needs of the visually impaired in mind, and can be used in in conjunction with Talk Back. Visual Toolbox presents two features that can be used by visually impaired individuals to better understand their environment. For example, the light Ssensor can be used to guide a visually impaired person to a light that has been left on, while the colour sensor can help them sort their white and dark clothes before doing laundry. Ultimately a project proposal, two progress reports, and a final report were written. Team meetings were scheduled for twice a week. A proposal presentation was given to the project manager, and the prototype was presented to group of students and three judges. The final result was presented to the class that included a demo and a pitch to sell the product to a group of students.

  • Client: Queen's University
  • Date: Nov-Dec 2015
  • Service: App Development

Bike Design

A wooden bike was developed by a team of 4 students at Queen's University as a design for a first-year design course. We were given a client, Queen's Biomechanical Research Group, who expected the final prototype to meet the following specifications:

  • Made from environmentally friendly materials (completely from wood if possible),
  • Cost-effective materials,
  • Weight limit greater than the average human,
  • Comfortable for a rider taller than 6’.

From these specifications, we decided that we would build a road bike. This meant that the materials used must be very light, and that the rider would be expected to ride for a long period of time, meaning that we had to position the handlebars accordingly. It was a general consensus that the group would use wood for the frame, but a more comfortable seat would be used, and it was assumed that the wheels would be pre-manufactured. Note that there are not pedals or chain to propel the bike. We felt this was outside of our scope as we dedicated much of our time to modelling the bike rather than creating a prototype. The bike complies with safety regulations as there is a light reflector on the front and back of the bike.
The final report detailed:

  • Force analysis of an ideal model,
  • Solid Edge model,
  • Detailed project timeline,
  • Economic breakdown (including the final cost of all materials).

Ultimately a project proposal, two progress reports, and a final report were written. Team meetings were scheduled for twice a week, and an additional meeting was scheduled with our project manager. A proposal presentation was given to the project manager, and our client, and the prototype was presented to group of students and three judges.

  • Client: Queen's Biomechanical Research Group
